# Agate Formation: Physics, Chemistry, and Algorithmic Implications

A research brief aimed at a technical artist simulating agate growth. Written April 2026. Conclusions
flagged as either established, contested, or speculative. Citations link to the primary sources used.

---

## 1. Primary formation mechanisms for agate

Agate is banded chalcedony — a fibrous aggregate of nano- to micro-crystalline alpha-quartz
intergrown with 1–20% moganite (a monoclinic SiO2 polymorph) and a variable amount of structurally
bound water. Bands are defined by switches in fiber orientation (length-fast chalcedony vs.
length-slow quartzine), fiber packing density, grain size (fiber vs. granular microquartz vs.
macrocrystalline quartz), and trace-element content (Al, Fe, Na, K, and others). Agate bodies form
inside cavities in volcanic rocks (vesicles, lithophysae) and, less commonly, in sedimentary
cavities, fractures, or geodes.

The field has two classic schools and a growing third:

**(a) Sequential infill from dilute aqueous silica (Heaney 1993, and most cathodoluminescence/
fluid-inclusion studies).** Peter Heaney argued that chalcedony precipitates from only
*mildly* supersaturated aqueous silica at <100 °C via spiral growth around a screw
dislocation with Burgers vector b = (n/2)[110]. Under this model, repeated solution pulses or
oscillating saturation states deliver silica to the cavity, and each band corresponds to a
distinct deposition event rather than a self-organized rhythm. This model explains fiber elongation
along [110] rather than [001], periodic fiber twisting about [110], the high density of Brazil-law
twin composition planes, and the routine intergrowth of moganite in chalcedony.
([Heaney 1993, *Contributions to Mineralogy and Petrology*](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF00712979);
[Heaney preprint PDF](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Peter-Heaney-3/publication/226626549_A_proposed_mechanism_for_the_growth_of_chalcedony/links/5425c4100cf238c6ea77779b/A-proposed-mechanism-for-the-growth-of-chalcedony.pdf))

**(b) Self-organization from a silica gel (Wang & Merino 1990; Merino, Wang & Deloule 1995).**
Wang and Merino modeled agate as the product of closed-system crystallization from a near-solid
amorphous silica lump (>=1 g SiO2/cm^3 — about two orders of magnitude above aqueous solubility).
In their reaction-transport framework, coupling of silica polymerization, cation uptake (mainly
Al3+), and diffusion spontaneously generates rhythmic bands without any external oscillation.
Fibers that incorporate more Al grow twisted (because Al3+ is larger than Si4+ and orders in the
lattice); low-Al fibers grow straight. This produces the characteristic alternation of twisted
length-fast chalcedony with straight or length-slow layers. See the ADS record and the AJS follow-ups.
([Wang & Merino 1990, *Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta*](https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/1990GeCoA..54.1627W);
[Merino, Wang & Deloule 1995, AJS](https://ajsonline.org/article/60699))

**(c) Age-dependent polymorph evolution via a metastable precursor (Moxon and co-workers,
1996–present).** Moxon's empirical work on Scottish, Brazilian, New Zealand, and other suites
argues that every agate starts as an amorphous silica / opal-A gel, then passes through opal-CT
and moganite, and finally matures toward microquartz. Moganite content, chalcedony crystallite
size, and bound water decrease with age of the agate (which is significantly younger than its
host lava). Brazilian Paraná agates form ~70–90 Myr *after* their 135 Ma basalt host; the horizontal
Uruguay bands in the same nodules are still younger. This frames agate formation as a very slow
diagenetic process rather than an event contemporaneous with lava emplacement.
([Moxon & Rios, Brazilian horizontal banding, *Min. Mag.* 2013](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/mineralogical-magazine/article/abs/characterization-and-genesis-of-horizontal-banding-in-brazilian-agate-an-xray-diffraction-thermogravimetric-and-electron-microprobe-study/6B251A083EF5DDA89FBC95E345A87F1D);
[*Agate Genesis: A Continuing Enigma*, 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-163X/10/11/953);
[Moxon's overview in *Deposits Mag*](https://depositsmag.com/2018/03/27/agate-a-mineral-that-develops-with-age-water-and-moganite/))

**(d) Recent reviews and syntheses (2020–2025).** Götze et al.'s 2020 review in *Minerals* is the
standard modern reference and concludes that agate micro-structure develops from silica sols /
amorphous silica precursors through spherulitic nucleation, not direct precipitation of quartz from
solution. Götze accepts that multiple mechanisms operate in parallel and co-exist in a single
specimen. ([Götze, Möckel & Pan, 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-163X/10/11/1037)). Cartwright
and collaborators (2024–2025) place agates into a broader class of "self-organized geological soft
matter" alongside Liesegang rings, chemical gardens, and concretions.
([Cartwright et al. 2024, arXiv:2412.18999](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.18999);
[Barge et al. 2024, *Minerals* 14:203 — chemically oscillating reactions as a model for agate geodes](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-163X/14/2/203);
[Cartwright et al. 2012/2013 *Phil. Trans. R. Soc. A*](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2012.0356))

**Current consensus, to the extent there is one:** agate bands are *not* the product of external
oscillations in a refilling fluid. They are overwhelmingly interpreted as self-organized rhythmic
precipitation inside a gel or highly supersaturated silica body, driven by the coupling of silica
polymerization, competition with moganite, impurity uptake, and diffusion. Heaney's spiral-growth
model remains the best explanation for the *fiber-scale* structure, whereas Wang–Merino / Liesegang
/ Ostwald-cycle models are the best explanation for the *band-scale* rhythm.

**Environment maps to pattern:**

- Volcanic vesicles in subaerial basalt flows (Brazil, Uruguay, Scotland, Germany) — classic
  wall-lining fortification agate on top, horizontal Uruguay banding on the bottom of the same
  cavity when gravity-settled micro-spherulites pile up.
- Lithophysae in rhyolite / ignimbrite (Mexico, Oregon) — plume, sagenite, and moss-agate
  habits with sparser banding; more tube and pseudomorph features.
- Sedimentary nodules and concretions (Lake Superior, Dryhead, Fairburn) — tight wall-lining
  banding and iron-oxide chromophore dominance.
- Thundereggs (spherulitic cavities in perlitic rhyolite) — star-shaped or fortification
  outline controlled by the original spherulite shape, then wet-lined by chalcedony.
- Silcretes / hot-spring sinters — cristobalite/opal-CT precursors, often moss or plume with weak
  banding.

## 2. Enumerated parameters and their effects

For each variable: physical meaning, representative range, what it changes visually,
and how strongly it controls the outcome.

1. **Silica concentration / supersaturation (strongest band-rhythm control).** Aqueous silica
   solubility at 25 °C is ~60–130 ppm (amorphous) and ~6 ppm (alpha-quartz). Wang–Merino require
   the equivalent of >=1 g SiO2/cm^3 to sustain self-organized rhythm, which is only realistic
   inside a gel. Heaney's screw-dislocation growth needs only *slight* supersaturation. Band
   sharpness, band count, and first-band position all follow from the ratio of supersaturation to
   nucleation threshold. (Götze 2020; Wang & Merino 1990.)

2. **Temperature (strong). Second-most important variable.** Agate forms below ~200–250 °C and
   typically 50–150 °C; high-T above ~270 °C promotes direct quartz and kills chalcedony fibers.
   Silica solubility is strongly temperature-dependent (Fournier's amorphous-silica solubility
   expression). Cooling rate controls how many supersaturation–depletion cycles the system can
   execute before the gel solidifies; slow cooling favors many thin bands, fast cooling favors
   few thick ones. (Götze 2020; [Fournier / Gunnarsson-Arnórsson amorphous silica solubility
   model](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0016703799004263))

3. **Pressure (weak to moderate).** Below ~50 MPa for agate; mainly sets the fluid phase (vapor
   vs. liquid) and the silica polymorph that nucleates first. Rarely the dominant control, but
   explains why deep hydrothermal veins give jasperoid or macroquartz rather than banded agate.

4. **pH (moderate, indirect).** Silica solubility is flat from pH 2–8 and rises sharply above
   pH 9. Most agate-forming systems are near-neutral to mildly alkaline. pH governs the balance
   between monomeric H4SiO4 and polymerized polysilicic species, which in turn controls whether
   a gel forms at all and how fast it dewaters. (USGS silica-in-hot-spring-waters reports.)

5. **Aluminum content of the source basalt or fluid (very strong — controls fiber twisting and
   color bands).** Al3+ substitutes for Si4+ in the tetrahedral site, needing a charge-balancing
   cation (H+, Na+, K+). The Wang–Merino model links Al uptake *directly* to fiber twisting,
   which is visible optically as the characteristic sweeping extinction of chalcedony under
   crossed polars. Al-rich bands tend to be darker and more translucent; Al-poor bands lean
   granular-white. (Wang & Merino 1990; Merino et al. 1995.)

6. **Iron (strong — the primary chromophore).** Fe3+ in oxidizing conditions gives reds,
   oranges, and yellow-browns via nano-inclusions of hematite, goethite, or akaganeite. Fe2+ in
   reduced conditions gives green or bluish tints. Fe is the single most visible trace element
   in agate. (Götze 2020; multiple *Mineralogy and Petrology* papers on chalcedony colouration.)

7. **Manganese (strong for dendrites / moss / black bands).** Mn4+ in MnO2/Mn-oxide dendrites
   gives black or dark-brown branching inclusions whose fractal geometry is genuinely DLA-like.
   Mn3+/Mn2+ disseminated in the silica matrix gives pink to violet. Mn oxide dendrites form
   after the main silica infill, along fractures, by solution flow — they are the one place
   where DLA is a quantitatively accurate model.

8. **Other chromophores (moderate).** Cr3+ gives chrome green (chrysoprase, prase).
   Ni2+ gives apple-green in Mg-rich chrysoprase. Ti4+ gives blue only in a few rare
   specimens. Cu in chrysocolla-agate gives teal. Organic or carbonaceous matter gives dark
   browns and is common in moss/sedimentary agate.

9. **Diffusion coefficients of dissolved silica and cations (strong — sets band spacing).**
   D ~ 1e-9 m^2/s for H4SiO4 in dilute water, dropping one to two orders of magnitude in a gel.
   In Liesegang-style models, band spacing scales with sqrt(D*t). Lower D (stiffer gel) → tighter
   bands; higher D (loose gel) → wider bands.

10. **Nucleation density on the cavity wall (strong — fortification vs. smooth).** Wall-lining
    fortification banding arises from many independent spherulitic nuclei on the cavity wall
    that collide and interlock. Sparse nucleation gives smooth concentric shells; dense
    nucleation gives jagged piecewise-linear (zigzag) sectors separated by compromise boundaries.
    (Götze 2020; [Grounded Lifestyles article summarizing the mechanism](https://www.groundedlifestyles.com/fortification-agate-formation/).)

11. **Cavity geometry (strong — sets the visible outline).** Vesicle aspect ratio,
    aperture size, and surface roughness set the shape of the outermost band. Bands then warp
    inward under volume-conservative inward migration; for irregular cavities, this means the
    interior bands have lower-frequency deformation than the rim (they have had more growth steps
    to smooth out).

12. **Fluid infiltration direction (strong for Uruguay-type, weak for wall-lining).**
    Where silica gel enters horizontally and settles under gravity, opal/micro-quartz spherulites
    sediment to the floor and produce flat horizontal laminae (Uruguay banding), often while
    wall-lining chalcedony grows on the ceiling and walls from a different source. Opal-C is
    the typical matrix in Uruguay bands and is absent from wall-lining bands.
    ([Petranek & Pouba on Uruguay-type agates](http://www.geology.cz/bulletin/fulltext/bullgeosci200404195.pdf);
    [Moxon & Rios 2013](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/mineralogical-magazine/article/abs/characterization-and-genesis-of-horizontal-banding-in-brazilian-agate-an-xray-diffraction-thermogravimetric-and-electron-microprobe-study/6B251A083EF5DDA89FBC95E345A87F1D))

13. **Cooling / evaporation / uplift rate (moderate).** Dewatering and cooling drive
    supersaturation oscillations. Field age data (Moxon) shows realistic rates are
    10 Myr / mm to 1 Myr / mm, not minutes or years. This is almost a geological diagenetic
    timescale, not an igneous timescale.

14. **Oscillation period (for cyclic-fluid models, weak and contested).** Classic "silica pulse"
    models invoke pulses of silica-charged fluid on 10^3–10^6 year cycles. Modern work largely
    rejects this as the dominant mechanism for fine banding; it may still operate for
    Uruguay-type horizontal layering where the cavity is refilled episodically.

15. **Host-rock pore size and permeability (moderate — controls which cavities become agate at
    all).** Ignimbrites and vesicular basalts have ~10^1–10^2 micrometre vesicles and provide the
    low-permeability seal required to retain a gel body long enough for banding to develop.

16. **Spherulite nucleation rate at the advancing front (strong — controls radial vs. fibrous
    layers).** A low nucleation rate gives long, straight chalcedony fibers (centimetres).
    A high nucleation rate gives granular microquartz "snow" bands. Alternation of these two
    regimes produces the classic translucent-vs-opaque band pairs.

17. **Chalcedony fiber orientation: length-fast vs. length-slow.** Length-fast chalcedony has its
    fiber axis along [1120] or [1010], perpendicular to [0001]; length-slow quartzine has its
    axis along [0001]. Switching between the two inside a single nodule often coincides with
    an abrupt band, and length-slow bands are preferentially associated with evaporitic or
    alkaline environments (chert in gypsum, sagenite). Feather agates are intergrown length-fast
    and length-slow domains. ([Miehe, Graetsch & Flörke 1984, *PCM* on length-fast crystal
    structure](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF00309311);
    [Götze 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-163X/10/11/1037))

18. **Moganite fraction (moderate — secular + environmental).** Recent samples may have 20–30
    wt% moganite in length-fast bands; old samples converge toward 0. Moganite reflects both
    age (Moxon's secular trend) and stabilisation of the crystallisation front; a stable front
    (length-slow quartzine) has less moganite than a jumpy front (length-fast chalcedony).
    ([*Scientific Reports* 2024, Bou Hamza agates](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-73136-x))

## 3. Mathematical models

### 3.1 Liesegang / periodic precipitation laws

These laws describe purely diffusion-reaction pattern formation in 1D from a moving front.
They provide the simplest quantitative backbone for banded agate.

- **Spacing law (Jablczynski 1923).** `x_{n+1} / x_n -> (1 + p)` as n grows, where p is a
  constant that depends on the concentrations of the inner/outer electrolytes. Band positions
  form a geometric series; each successive band is further from the seed than the last.
- **Time law (Morse & Pierce).** `x_n^2 ~ t_n` — the nth band position squared is proportional
  to the time at which it appeared. Direct consequence of diffusive transport.
- **Matalon–Packter law.** `p = F(b_0) + G(b_0) / a_0`, where a_0 is outer-electrolyte
  concentration and b_0 is inner-electrolyte concentration. The spacing coefficient drops as
  outer-electrolyte concentration rises. ([Review: Nabika et al. 2019, *Langmuir*](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acs.langmuir.9b03018);
  [Quasi-universal form for Fe(OH)2, 2023](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acs.langmuir.3c00778))
- **Width law.** Band width scales approximately with x_n^alpha, alpha ~ 0.9–1.0. Empirical.
- **Revert banding / anti-Liesegang.** In some regimes (high inner electrolyte, confined
  geometry) bands appear *closer together* with distance. Observed in both experiments and in
  some agate nodules. ([Lagzi et al. 2019, *ACS Omega*](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acsomega.9b00937))

### 3.2 Reaction-diffusion (Turing, Gray-Scott, Brusselator)

Write a system of two or more species U, V:

```
dU/dt = D_U * Lap(U) + f(U, V)
dV/dt = D_V * Lap(V) + g(U, V)
```

With the right kinetics these generate Turing patterns (spots, stripes, labyrinths).
For agate, a useful two-species system is *supersaturated silica S* and *polymerized /
precipitated silica P*, coupled through a nucleation threshold:

```
dS/dt = D_S * Lap(S) - k * (S - S_eq) * H(S - S_crit)
dP/dt =                k * (S - S_eq) * H(S - S_crit)
```

where H is the Heaviside step (precipitation only above the supersaturation threshold S_crit).
A moving supersaturation front that periodically spikes past S_crit produces Liesegang-type bands.
This is the skeleton of the Wang–Merino model and also of more recent reaction-diffusion-reaction
formulations. ([MDPI 2021, Prussian blue analogue RDR bands](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/11/11/5000);
[Frontiers 2022, RDR periodic patterns](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physics/articles/10.3389/fphy.2022.828444/full))

### 3.3 Wang–Merino state of the art

The 1990 paper and its 1995 AJS follow-ups are the textbook self-organization treatment.
Core equations (paraphrased):

- Silica polymerization is nonlinear in local [H4SiO4] and autocatalytic in existing polymer
  surface area → produces an excitable medium.
- Aluminum uptake is proportional to fiber surface / polymerization rate and modulates lattice
  strain → Al-rich growth is twisted, Al-poor growth is straight.
- Chalcedony / moganite / quartz fractions are governed by an Ostwald step-rule cascade where
  the metastable phase nucleates first, then recrystallises.

These combine into a reaction-transport system whose traveling fronts leave behind alternating
compositional lamellae without any external oscillation. It is *the* canonical self-organizational
model for agate and remains the state of the art for band-scale rhythm.
([Wang & Merino 1990 ADS record](https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/1990GeCoA..54.1627W);
[Merino et al. 1995 AJS fibers](https://ajsonline.org/article/60666))

### 3.4 Spherulite growth and fBM-like warping

A diffusion-controlled spherulite front in a silicate gel has its radius evolve as
`r(t) = beta * sqrt(D*t)` (Mullins–Sekerka / Avrami). When fronts from multiple seeds collide,
they form compromise boundaries (piecewise-convex curves that meet at cusps). The apparent
*warp* of agate bands in 3D is dominated by three effects:

- The outline of the cavity (geometric, low-frequency).
- Compromise boundaries between spherulites that nucleated on the cavity wall at slightly
  different times (piecewise-linear cusps — the "fortification" corners).
- Anisotropy in fiber bundle growth rate (length-fast vs length-slow domains). Locally this is
  well-approximated by fractional Brownian motion on the band surface, because the many
  independent spherulites on the front produce something close to self-affine roughness.

### 3.5 Stochastic models: DLA, Monte Carlo

DLA appears in the agate literature *only* for dendritic inclusions (Mn and Fe oxides)
growing on fractures after the main chalcedony mass has solidified. There is no serious
peer-reviewed model of the banded chalcedony itself as a DLA process. The physical basis is
wrong: DLA produces branching, fractal, open aggregates with no characteristic length, whereas
agate bands are dense, layered, and have a clear characteristic spacing set by D, S_crit, and
S. Monte Carlo pore-network simulations exist for the seepage stage (silica-charged fluid
infiltration through the host rock) but not for the banding itself.

### 3.6 Phase-field models

Phase-field approaches (Cahn–Hilliard + Allen–Cahn) are increasingly used for spherulite growth
in polymers and for crystallization fronts in silicate melts. They naturally produce
Mullins–Sekerka instabilities (the "fingers" that give rise to dendritic spherulites). For
agate they are underused but promising — you get band spacing, fiber orientation, and
compromise boundaries out of a single energy functional.

## 4. Characteristic features and their causes

- **Concentric wall-lining banding.** Spherulitic chalcedony nucleates densely on the vesicle
  wall and grows inward. Spherulites coalesce into a smooth front within a few microns. After
  that, self-organized rhythm (Liesegang or Wang–Merino) paints bands on the advancing front.
  ([Götze 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-163X/10/11/1037))

- **Fortification (zigzag) banding.** Wall-lining but with sparser or unevenly-spaced
  nucleation, so individual spherulites meet in piecewise-linear compromise boundaries that
  propagate inward as angular cusps. Every band inherits the corner, so you get the nested
  polygonal "fortress wall" look.
  ([Grounded Lifestyles](https://www.groundedlifestyles.com/fortification-agate-formation/);
  [Quartzpage.de agate page](http://www.quartzpage.de/agate.html))

- **Uruguay / horizontal banding.** Granular micro-quartz and opal-C spherulites sediment to the
  floor of a cavity under gravity while the silica sol is still mobile. Produces flat parallel
  layers independent of the cavity outline. Age-dating shows the Uruguay stage *precedes* the
  wall-lining stage in Brazilian agates by 10–20 Myr.
  ([Moxon & Rios 2013](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/mineralogical-magazine/article/abs/characterization-and-genesis-of-horizontal-banding-in-brazilian-agate-an-xray-diffraction-thermogravimetric-and-electron-microprobe-study/6B251A083EF5DDA89FBC95E345A87F1D);
  [Petranek & Pouba](http://www.geology.cz/bulletin/fulltext/bullgeosci200404195.pdf))

- **Moss agate.** Chlorite, hornblende, or Mn/Fe oxide inclusions suspended in an otherwise
  colorless silica matrix. The inclusions grow first (by solution transport / DLA along fractures)
  and are then entombed. ([Ganoksin article on chemically induced inclusions](https://www.ganoksin.com/article/dendrites-moss-plume-inclusions/))

- **Plume and sagenite.** Feathery or needle-like inclusions formed by metastable acicular
  minerals (rutile, goethite, stibnite) that nucleate in the silica gel before it solidifies.
  Plume differs from dendrite in that the growth is mineral-surface-controlled rather than
  diffusion-limited.

- **Eyes, tubes, stalactitic.** Eyes are cross-sections of stalactitic growths — a chalcedony
  tube nucleated on a pendant ridge or debris grain on the cavity ceiling and grew downward
  under gravity. When cut across, the nested bands look like a concentric eye.

- **Fibrous chalcedony vs. granular microquartz.** Fibrous chalcedony nucleates at low
  supersaturation on a stable front (length-fast if Al-present, length-slow if evaporitic).
  Granular microquartz nucleates at high supersaturation with many simultaneous nuclei; the
  grains never organize into fibers. Alternation of the two regimes is the main source of
  band-to-band optical contrast.

- **Translucent vs. opaque bands.** Fibrous chalcedony is translucent because all fibers are
  aligned and sub-wavelength in diameter — light passes with little scattering. Granular
  microquartz and quartz "snow" bands are opaque because grain boundaries scatter light at
  roughly the optical wavelength. Fluid-inclusion density adds to the milky appearance.

- **Sharp vs. diffuse band boundaries.** Sharp boundaries occur where the supersaturation drops
  *below* the nucleation threshold (no growth, then a hard restart when supersaturation recovers).
  Diffuse boundaries occur where growth continues through the transition with only a change in
  fiber type or impurity uptake. The sharpness is a diagnostic for whether a Liesegang-style
  intermittent mechanism was active.

## 5. Evaluation of the current DLA-based algorithm

**What the current algorithm gets right:**

- **Seeded growth with a preferred direction is qualitatively correct** for fibrous chalcedony:
  real fibers do have a strong c-axis (or [110]) preference, and real nucleation starts at
  specific points on the cavity wall. Biasing walkers toward the nearest crystal axis of the
  nearest seed captures this at a cartoon level.
- **Banding by distance from the seed is physically meaningful** *if* you interpret distance as
  a proxy for time elapsed since nucleation. The Liesegang time law `x_n^2 ~ t_n` and the
  spherulite growth law `r ~ sqrt(D*t)` both support "distance from seed = growth time" for the
  simplest geometries. Bucketing into variable-width bands approximates the Jablczynski geometric
  series; with a logarithmic bucket size you would get the textbook Liesegang spacing.
- **fBM warp of band boundaries is a reasonable surrogate** for the many-spherulite compromise
  boundaries. Agate bands in 3D really do have self-affine roughness, and fBM with H in ~0.6–0.8
  is a plausible first approximation.
- **Per-band color from a discrete palette** matches the discrete physical regimes: Al-rich
  twisted chalcedony, Al-poor straight chalcedony, quartzine, granular microquartz, opaque
  quartz "snow", Fe/Mn-impurity-stained band. Four to six categories is defensible.

**What is cosmetic or physically wrong:**

- **DLA random-walker aggregation is the wrong microphysics for chalcedony bulk.** Real silica
  deposition is a dense, front-advancing process in a gel — not a sparse, branching aggregation
  from a dilute vapor or electrolyte. The DLA fractal dimension (~1.71 in 2D) does not match
  the compact, layered structure of agate (dimension 2.0 in 2D).
- **DLA's noise-driven branching at the growth tip is absent in real agate bands.** Agate
  fronts are smooth or piecewise smooth (compromise boundaries). The only place DLA is
  quantitatively correct is for Mn/Fe oxide dendrites *inside* moss/plume agate.
- **Walker bias toward crystal axes is a decorative gloss, not a mechanism.** In real fibers,
  orientation is set by screw-dislocation geometry (Heaney) and by Al-induced twist (Wang–Merino),
  not by a random walker preferring a direction.
- **Uniform band-width buckets miss the Jablczynski geometric progression.** Real bands get
  wider with distance from the cavity wall in the Liesegang regime and narrower in the
  "revert" regime; a uniform or linearly-graded bucket captures neither.
- **Distance-from-seed does not distinguish wall-lining from Uruguay-type banding.** The
  horizontal-vs-concentric distinction is a first-class visual feature of real agates and is
  a consequence of the gravity vector, not of distance.

**Answers to the specific questions:**

- *Does real agate involve DLA at all?* Yes, but only for oxide dendrites in moss/plume/dendritic
  agate. The main banded chalcedony mass is not a DLA structure.
- *Would Liesegang be more accurate?* Yes, substantially, for the band rhythm.
- *Would spherulitic growth be more accurate?* Yes, for the microstructure and for fortification
  corners.
- *Would a hybrid (front propagation + oscillatory precipitation + impurity-driven color) be
  more accurate?* Yes. This is the Wang–Merino picture and essentially what modern papers
  recommend. It is also the best match for visual richness.

## 6. Algorithm proposals

### Proposal A — Liesegang front-propagation agate (recommended baseline)

A 2D or 3D eikonal front advancing inward from the cavity wall, with a 1D Liesegang-style
supersaturation / precipitation oscillator driving band births. This is the simplest accurate
model.

```
# Inputs: cavity mask, nucleation seeds on wall, D_silica, S_0, S_crit, S_eq, k_precip
# Outputs: per-voxel band index, fiber-type flag, Al uptake, color

initialize:
  distance field T(x) = 0 inside cavity, +inf outside
  for each nucleation seed on cavity wall: T(seed) = 0, set local fiber axis
  S(x) = S_0                 # silica supersaturation
  P(x) = 0                   # deposited polymer
  band_id(x) = -1

propagate front (Fast Marching or simple BFS with Jablczynski-weighted step):
  while active front:
    step dt
    update S via: dS/dt = D_silica * Lap(S) - k_precip * max(0, S - S_eq)
    for each voxel reached this step:
      if S(x) > S_crit:
        band_id(x) = current_band
        P(x) += precipitation
        S(x) -= depletion        # Ostwald depletion zone
      else:
        leave as boundary        # next band will start after recovery
    if global depletion > threshold: current_band += 1

post-process:
  assign fiber type from local (Al/S) ratio history
  assign color from band_id + fiber type + impurity field (separately sampled)
  warp band surfaces with fBM of moderate H
```

Key equations: diffusion PDE above; Jablczynski spacing `x_{n+1}/x_n = 1+p`;
time law `x_n^2 ~ t_n`; Matalon–Packter `p = F(b_0) + G(b_0)/a_0`.

Tradeoffs: gives geometrically-spaced bands for free, respects arbitrary cavity shapes,
cheap enough for interactive resolution. Tunability is excellent — a_0, b_0, S_crit, and D are
all intuitive knobs. Rendering of fibers still needs a separate shader.

### Proposal B — Spherulitic-seed growth with compromise boundaries

```
# Inputs: cavity mask, seed density on wall, fiber axis per seed, growth anisotropy
# Outputs: voronoi-like sector map, radial distance per sector, band structure per sector

place N seeds on cavity wall with density d
run anisotropic front propagation from all seeds simultaneously
  (Eikonal with direction-dependent speed F(x, n_hat) = v_fast + (v_slow - v_fast) * |n_hat . axis|)
when fronts meet -> compromise boundary (record)
for each voxel: assigned seed = earliest-arrival seed; radial = arrival time
overlay banding: band index = floor(radial / band_width(radial))
  with band_width(radial) proportional to radial (Liesegang geometric)
color: band_index -> palette bucket; occasional random-walk Mn dendrite overlay
```

Key equations: anisotropic eikonal `|grad T(x)| * F(x, grad T) = 1`; spherulite compromise
boundaries are the loci where two arrival times are equal.

Tradeoffs: this produces *fortification* banding naturally — change seed density on the wall
and you slide from smooth concentric to sharp zigzag. It is the most honest visual match for
volcanic agate. Slightly more expensive than Proposal A. Good reuse of your existing
per-seed and fBM code.

### Proposal C — Hybrid (Wang–Merino dynamics, recommended for an art-quality result)

Use Proposal B's spherulitic front geometry, and on top of it run a reaction-transport solver
for two fields (silica supersaturation S and Al concentration A). Impurity bands come from
rhythmic Al uptake, twist from local A gradient, Uruguay horizontal layers from a second
lower-cavity reservoir with gravity-driven deposition. Mn/Fe oxide dendrites are a
post-process DLA layer in the moss-agate mode.

Pseudocode:

```
simulate spherulitic front (Proposal B) and record arrival time T(x) and sector(x)
on the advancing surface:
  integrate coupled:
    dS/dt = D_S * Lap(S) - k_s * (S - S_eq) * H(S - S_crit) - k_al * (A - A_eq)
    dA/dt = D_A * Lap(A) - k_al * (A - A_eq) * H(S - S_crit)
  record S_trace(x), A_trace(x) = history of (S, A) at the moment x crystallized
fiber type(x):
  if A_trace(x) > A_twist_threshold: length_fast + twisted
  elif environment == evaporitic:   length_slow quartzine
  else:                             length_fast straight
color(x):
  base = palette_from_S_cycle(S_trace(x))
  tint = impurity_field(x) * Fe_Mn_mask(x)
  final = blend(base, tint)
Uruguay stage (optional):
  fill lower cavity fraction with horizontal granular-microquartz laminae before front arrival
Mn dendrite post-process (moss mode):
  run 2D DLA along fracture paths inside final solid; burn into color field
```

Key equations: Wang–Merino reaction-transport as above; Liesegang scaling for band births;
anisotropic eikonal for the front; DLA for oxide dendrites only.

Tradeoffs: most physically complete, most tunable, most work to implement. GPU compute (TypeGPU)
is a good fit — the two fields S and A each fit a storage texture, and the front can be
represented as a level set. Expect minutes of precomputation for a detailed 3D nodule; one-time
cost.

**Code reuse from the current DLA version:**

- Seed placement, cavity geometry, and per-seed axis logic map directly to Proposal B.
- fBM warp of band boundaries remains useful as a cheap post-process for adding surface
  roughness to the spherulite compromise surfaces.
- The discrete palette / band-color category system transfers as-is; just drive it from
  `band_id + fiber_type + impurity` instead of distance buckets.
- Your existing random-walker DLA implementation becomes the Mn/Fe dendrite post-process for
  moss/plume mode rather than the primary growth algorithm. Don't throw it away — use it for
  what it is actually physically correct for.
